The Six Models02
Token Value Accrual Taxonomy

1. Direct fee distribution

Protocol revenue flows directly to token stakers as yield. The closest analog to equity dividends. Investors receive real, non-inflationary returns denominated in the protocol's fee currency.
dYdX (100% fees to stakers) · GMX (ETH/AVAX yield to stakers)

2. Buyback-and-burn

Protocol uses revenue to purchase tokens on the open market and permanently remove them from circulation. Reduces supply over time. The closest analog to share repurchases in traditional equity markets.
Meteora ($10M program) · Orca (20% of fees) · Raydium (12% of fees, $196M cumulative)

3. Staking revenue share

Holders lock tokens and receive a share of protocol revenue proportional to their stake. Differs from direct distribution by requiring active participation and time-lock commitment.
Pendle (80% revenue to vePENDLE + 3% yield fees)

4. Conditional buyback

Buyback activation is tied to protocol health metrics. Prevents value extraction during downturns. The most sophisticated governance design, but hardest to explain to allocators.
Lido NEST (ETH >$3K, revenue >$40M/yr, $10M/yr cap)

5. ve-model epoch distribution

100% of protocol fees distributed to vote-escrowed token holders each epoch. Creates economic flywheel through bribes. Requires active management, limiting passive institutional adoption.
Aerodrome (100% fees to veAERO voters)

6. Governance-only (no fee switch)

Token grants governance rights but no economic claim on protocol revenue. $0 flows to holders regardless of protocol profitability. The weakest model from an institutional perspective.
Compound ($0 to holders) · Morpho ($0 despite $3.8B TVL) · Kamino ($0 despite $1.7B TVL)

Finding

Only Hyperliquid (+193%) and Aerodrome (+5%) posted positive 1-year returns. Both have active value accrual mechanisms. But the remaining buyback protocols (Aave, Jupiter, Raydium) all declined -30% to -59%, proving that buybacks alone don't guarantee outperformance. The worst performer with the best VA score: dYdX at -82%. Revenue scale matters more than mechanism design.

Alpha signal

The data suggests a weak but directional relationship: protocols with active value accrual mechanisms (buyback, ve-model, direct distribution) outperformed governance-only tokens by an average of 19 percentage points over the past year. But within the active group, execution quality and revenue scale drive the variance. Hyperliquid's +193% return is a revenue story ($1.74M/day), not a mechanism story. Strip out Hyperliquid, and the average buyback return drops to -45%.

What Actually Drives Returns05

Revenue scale beats mechanism design

Hyperliquid generates $1.74M in daily revenue and returned +193% over the past year. Raydium generates $20K daily with a well-designed buyback program and returned -59%. The buyback mechanism is identical in principle. The difference is three orders of magnitude in revenue. Institutional allocators should evaluate the revenue engine first, the accrual mechanism second.

$1.74M/day (HYPE) vs. $20K/day (RAY) with same model class

Governance-only is a dead model

Compound (-48%) and Kamino (-54%) both have zero value flowing to holders. Compound trades at a 36x FDV/rev multiple despite $5.3M in annualized revenue. Morpho has $3.8B in TVL and Apollo buying 9% of supply, yet $0 reaches the token. The market is increasingly punishing protocols that generate revenue without distributing it. The average governance-only return was -51% over the past year.

Governance-only avg: -51% · Active accrual avg: -32%

Buybacks work, but only at scale

Crypto protocols spent over $1.4B on buybacks in 2025 (CoinGecko data). Hyperliquid alone accounted for 46% of that total. Jupiter spent $70M on buybacks and is still down -55% from a year ago. Helium paused its buyback program entirely after seeing no market impact. The research from Keyrock and altFINS confirms: buybacks are most effective when executed at low P/S ratios with revenue that exceeds unlock-driven sell pressure. Most protocols fail that test.

$1.4B spent on buybacks in 2025 · most tokens still down

IR doesn't make your token go up. IR makes your token investable by the kind of capital that sustains long-term value.

Bottom line

There is no universally "best" model. The answer depends on revenue scale, protocol maturity, and target investor base. But the data is clear on one thing: governance-only tokens with no value accrual are the worst-performing class. Any active mechanism, executed at sufficient revenue scale, outperforms doing nothing. The alpha is not in the mechanism. The alpha is in the revenue.
